Research

Multimodal AI methods for complex diseases

The Gevaert Lab has an interdisciplinary focus on developing novel algorithms for multi-scale biomedical data fusion. We work at the intersection of machine learning, genomics, imaging, and clinical medicine — primarily in oncology and cardiovascular disease.

Flagship Project

Medical Digital Twin

We are developing a medical digital twin — a computational model that integrates a patient's multi-scale data (genomics, imaging, clinical records, wearables) to create a personalized virtual replica that can simulate disease trajectories and predict treatment responses.

This work, published in Lancet Digital Health (2025), proposes a framework for how AI can move from single-modality prediction to holistic patient modeling for precision medicine and medical AI.

Deep Learning

Computational Pathology

We develop deep learning models to analyze whole-slide histopathology images for cancer diagnosis, subtype classification, and survival prediction. Our work links pathology phenotypes with molecular data (genomics, epigenomics) to reveal multi-scale disease mechanisms.

Recent work includes predicting spatial gene expression from H&E slides using linearized attention (Nature Communications, 2024) and generating synthetic pathology slide tiles from RNA-seq data using diffusion models (Nature Biomedical Engineering, 2025).

Imaging Genomics

Quantitative Imaging & Radiogenomics

We develop a framework for non-invasive personalized medicine by linking radiomic features extracted from CT, MR, and PET images with genomic data. This imaging genomics (or radiogenomics) approach reveals how tumor imaging phenotypes reflect underlying molecular biology.

Key results include LungNet — a shallow CNN that predicts lung cancer survival from multi-institutional CT data (Nature Machine Intelligence cover, 2020) — and deep learning models for brain tumor segmentation achieving >90% Dice scores.

Epigenomics

Cancer Epigenomics

Aberrant DNA methylation is a key mechanism in oncogenesis. We developed MethylMix, a computational algorithm to identify differentially methylated genes that are also transcriptionally predictive. Applied across 12 cancer types plus pancancer analyses, MethylMix discovered novel methylation-driven subgroups with clinical relevance.

We also developed AMARETTO and pancancer AMARETTO, which integrate copy number, DNA methylation, and gene expression data to identify cancer driver genes and regulatory modules across cancers.

Data Fusion

Multi-omics Data Fusion

A central theme of the lab is developing methods for integrating multiple layers of biological data. We pioneered early data fusion work using Bayesian and kernel methods in breast and ovarian cancer, and have since developed deep learning approaches for multimodal data fusion.

Recent work includes synthetic multimodal data modelling for data imputation (Nature Biomedical Engineering, 2025), sparse canonical correlation analysis for COVID-19 cohort studies (NPJ Digital Medicine, 2024), and multimodal deep learning for prognosis in brain tumors.

Meta-Learning

Meta-Learning for Oncology

Biomedical AI is often limited by small sample sizes. We develop meta-learning approaches that leverage knowledge from large source-domain datasets to improve models in data-scarce target domains — reducing the amount of data needed by an order of magnitude.

In one key study, we used meta-learning to predict cancer patient survival from genomic data (Nature Communications, 2020). We also developed GeNNius, a graph neural network method for ultra-fast drug-target interaction inference (Bioinformatics, 2024).

Cancer Biology

NSD1 Inactivation & Immune Cold Tumors

We characterized a DNA hypomethylated subtype of lung squamous cell carcinoma defined by NSD1 inactivation, showing strong overlap with a similar subtype in head and neck squamous cell carcinoma. This subtype displays an "immune cold" phenotype with low leukocyte infiltration and low PD-1/PD-L1 expression.

These findings have direct implications for immunotherapy selection and reveal a general role for NSD1 in maintaining epigenetic repression of immune-related genes in the tumor microenvironment.

NCI · CTD²

Cancer Target Discovery & Development

We are active members of the NCI Cancer Target Discovery and Development (CTD²) network, collaborating with the labs of Calvin Kuo, Christina Curtis, and Hanlee Ji at Stanford. Our focus is on identifying hypomethylated oncogene candidates using MethylMix and its extensions.

Oncogenes activated by hypomethylation have been understudied relative to gene repression by hypermethylation, and represent promising therapeutic targets. All network data is available via the CTD² Data Portal.
